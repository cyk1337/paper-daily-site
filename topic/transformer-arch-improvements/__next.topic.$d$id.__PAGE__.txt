4:T54c,This report addresses a narrow question: by 2026, which architectural changes for dense Transformer backbones still deserve investment, and which ones are already close to settled. Public evidence has largely converged on three composable paths. First is KV-cache cost reduction on the attention side: from MHA to GQA, then local/global hybrids with SWA, and more aggressive MLA in larger models or MoE settings. Second is the default recipe for stability and throughput: pre-RMSNorm, SwiGLU, QK-Norm, plus sandwich norm in some model reports. Third is not “replace attention,” but “replace the growth path”: starting from a stable base and using depth-up-scaling or block expansion under continued pretraining, instead of retraining a larger model from scratch every time. The key question is not whether an idea is new, but whether it yields a reproducible benefit-to-cost ratio under a given scale, context length, and serving constraint. The literature is already enough to support an executable checklist: for dense models below 70B, default to GQA first; for long-context serving, prioritize local/global interleaving; for dense models at or above 200B or MoE, consider MLA; and when a strong base already exists and the target size is roughly below 30B, evaluate depth-up-scaling or block expansion before committing to from-scratch scaling.5:T54d,Public evidence supports a practical conclusion: in 2026, effort should no longer be spread across many new attention variants. For most dense LLMs, the highest marginal returns still come from three composable upgrades. First, GQA is the default below 70B because it preserves most quality while compressing KV-cache relative to MHA; if the serving target is long context such as 128K, SWA or local-global interleaving is more cost-effective than all-global attention, with Gemma 3 [Gemma3Report] reporting a 5:1 recipe and roughly 4× lower inference memory; if the model is already at or above 200B or uses MoE, MLA in DeepSeek-V2 [DeepSeekV2] reduces KV to about one-seventh while staying near GQA-level quality. Second, Norm/FFN choices have mostly converged to pre-RMSNorm + SwiGLU, and QK-Norm now has sufficiently strong mechanistic evidence for spike control at scale [Wortsman2023Instabilities], though sandwich norm still lacks broad independent ablations. Third, scaling up is no longer only a from-scratch story: SOLAR [Kim2023Solar] and LLaMA Pro [LLaMAPro2024] show that when a stable base already exists, depth-up-scaling or block expansion can be cheaper than retraining. The more durable reading is to treat “architecture innovation” as component composition plus scaling-path selection, not as a search for yet another backbone label.6:T5b4,The conclusion first: over the past three years, the attention-side upgrades that actually stuck are all about KV-cache cost, not theoretical training complexity. Shazeer [Shazeer2019MQA] framed the problem clearly: in decoder inference, the bottleneck is often not computing Q, but repeatedly writing and reading per-layer, per-head KV. Ainslie et al. [Ainslie2023GQA] rebut the idea that “saving KV means using MQA” because MQA ties all query heads to a single KV head and often loses quality; GQA introduces grouped sharing as a middle point, preserving most quality while capturing most of the memory gain. Mistral 7B [Mistral2023] then rebuts the claim that long context requires fully global attention: many token dependencies are local, so SWA with a rolling buffer turns globally accumulating KV into mostly local-window storage in most layers. Gemma 3 [Gemma3Report] operationalizes this line: a 5-local, 1-global interleave yields roughly 4× lower memory at 128K inference, while QK-Norm and sandwich norm keep training stable. DeepSeek-V2 [DeepSeekV2] pushes KV savings further through latent compression: instead of storing fewer heads, it stores a low-dimensional latent and reconstructs per-head KV, with public numbers around one-seventh of MHA. The consensus is shifting: the main question in attention design is no longer an O(n^2) slogan, but per-token cache cost, bandwidth pressure, and deployability [Fu2024PeakPerf][InfiniteLLM2024].7:T568,If “architecture improvement” is decomposed, the Norm/FFN line is far less contested than attention. RMSNorm already offered a lower-cost normalization alternative in Zhang and Sennrich [RMSNorm2019], and Jiang et al. [Jiang2023PreRMSNorm] further show that within pre-norm Transformers it plays a role close to Pre-LN, making continued LayerNorm benchmarking low-yield. The FFN side is similar: after Shazeer [Shazeer2020GLU], gated FFNs such as SwiGLU largely dominate standard GELU FFNs on quality per parameter. The real unsaturated part is QK-Norm and sandwich norm. Wortsman et al. [Wortsman2023Instabilities] rebut the idea that loss spikes are mainly solved by learning-rate tuning and clipping, because they localize the issue to attention-logit variance growth and output-norm growth, and show that components such as QK-Norm and z-loss can suppress spikes reproducibly. Gemma 3 [Gemma3Report] continues with QK-Norm and sandwich norm, but the evidence should be separated: QK-Norm now has mechanistic support plus reuse across reports, while sandwich norm is still mostly report-level practice without broad independent decoder-only ablations across scales. A more practical reading is: RMSNorm + SwiGLU can be treated as defaults; QK-Norm should be enabled first in large-scale training and long-context recipes; sandwich norm remains “recommended but not closed.”8:T633,Kaplan et al. [Kaplan2020ScalingLaws] suggest an intuition: if parameters, data, and compute follow scaling laws, architectural details are mostly secondary. That reading still holds for new models, new data, and ample budgets, but it does not imply that every larger model should be trained from scratch. SOLAR [Kim2023Solar] rebuts that implication by showing a concrete alternative path: start from a stable 7B base, duplicate layers for depth-up-scaling, then continue pretraining for 200B tokens to obtain a 10.7B model that beats comparable from-scratch baselines. LLaMA Pro [LLaMAPro2024] shows that if the goal is domain continued pretraining rather than rebuilding general capability, block expansion is often safer: insert identity-initialized layers, train only the new ones, and preserve base capability better. Learning to Grow [Wang2023Grow], Masked Structural Growth [Yao2023MSG], and the earlier Net2Net [Chen2015Net2Net] all support a shared assumption: optimization trajectory and learned representation are transferable assets, not something that must be reset at every scale jump. Allen-Zhu and Li [AllenZhu2024Physics] add a mechanism: in some knowledge-storage regimes, depth improves extractable capacity more than width. The steadier recommendation is not “never train from scratch again,” but to rewrite the question as: do you already have a stable base, is the target roughly ≤30B, is the data domain-skewed, and is the continued-pretraining token budget sufficient? If the first three are true, growth paths should enter the shortlist before retraining.9:T5b1,Two opposing camps deserve serious treatment. The first says “architecture is mostly done; keep scaling data and recipes.” LLaMA [LLaMA2023], Llama 2 [Llama2Report], Llama 3 [Llama3Report], and Data Engineering for 128K [Fu2024DataEngineering] all support this line: many capability gains do come from longer training, cleaner data, and better curricula rather than new attention labels. The issue is not that this camp is factually wrong, but that it can underweight serving cost. Gemma 3 [Gemma3Report], Mistral 7B [Mistral2023], and DeepSeek-V2 [DeepSeekV2] rebut it because once context reaches 128K and models move beyond 70B, KV-cache is no longer an implementation detail but a deployment constraint. The second camp says “the next backbone should move to SSM / RetNet / Mamba-like models.” RetNet [RetNet2023] is directionally plausible: if a new backbone can preserve training parallelism, reduce inference-state cost, and match language-model quality under equal budgets, then Transformer is no longer the only option. But public evidence still lacks enough matched-budget decoder-only LM comparisons, especially ones aligning perplexity, throughput, and long-context utilization quality at once. Combined with the lesson from Lost in the Middle [LostMiddle2023]—that fitting long context is not the same as using middle-context evidence well—the steadier move is still to exhaust mature Transformer recipes before switching backbones.a:T9f7,可操作清单：
1. 稠密 decoder-only ≤70B，默认 GQA，不要再把 MHA 当主候选；KV 分组先从 h_kv = h/8 或邻近档位起扫。原因是 Ainslie et al. [Ainslie2023GQA] 已经说明 GQA 拿到接近 MQA 的 KV 收益，同时比 MQA 更稳；Shazeer [Shazeer2019MQA] 适合作为极端压缩下界，而不是默认项。
2. 目标上下文 ≥128K 且服务成本敏感，优先 local/global 混合，不要直接 all-global。先试 Gemma 3 [Gemma3Report] 的 5:1 交错或 Mistral 7B [Mistral2023] 式 SWA，再看任务是否真的需要更多全局层。否则你会先把显存和带宽打满，再去讨论质量。
3. MLA 不要在小模型上为了“先进”而硬上。更合适的边界是 MoE 或稠密 ≥200B，再考虑 DeepSeek-V2 [DeepSeekV2] 这类 latent KV 路线；同时预留 decoupled RoPE、kernel 与 serving 栈改造成本。若团队没有这部分系统能力，GQA/SWA 的性价比更高。
4. Norm / FFN 直接收敛到 pre-RMSNorm + SwiGLU。不要再为 LayerNorm、GELU、post-norm 做大规模 benchmark，除非你的目标是复现实验史。依据是 Zhang and Sennrich [RMSNorm2019]、Jiang et al. [Jiang2023PreRMSNorm] 与 Shazeer [Shazeer2020GLU] 的共同结论。
5. QK-Norm 在两个场景优先打开：一是训练规模上去后出现 attention-logit spike；二是长上下文 continued pretraining。Wortsman et al. [Wortsman2023Instabilities] 与 Gemma 3 [Gemma3Report] 已足够支持这条经验。不要把它期待成“任何规模都加分”的万能组件。
6. 若已有稳定 base、目标规模 ≤30B、continued pretrain token 预算在 50B–300B 量级，先评估 depth-up-scaling 或 block expansion，再决定是否 from-scratch。Solar [Kim2023Solar] 与 LLaMA Pro [LLaMAPro2024] 说明这条路常常更省。反过来，如果是全新架构或全新大规模数据路线，直接按 scaling law 规划 from-scratch 更干净 [Kaplan2020ScalingLaws][Llama3Report]。
7. ablation 不要只做单点。至少做 3 个规模点、2 个上下文长度、并同时报训练稳定性、KV-cache、吞吐与下游质量。否则很容易把数据工程收益误记成架构收益，或把服务收益误记成训练收益 [Fu2024DataEngineering][Fu2024PeakPerf]。
8. open — 证据不足的地方要明确标注：sandwich norm 是否必要、QK-Norm 是否在稳定性之外持续带来收益、以及 Transformer 对 SSM/RetNet 的 matched-budget 优劣，目前都还没有足够干净的公开结论 [Gemma3Report][Wortsman2023Instabilities][RetNet2023]。b:Tb5a,An executable checklist:
1. For dense decoder-only models at or below 70B, default to GQA and stop treating MHA as the main candidate; start the KV grouping sweep around h_kv = h/8 or nearby ratios. Ainslie et al. [Ainslie2023GQA] already show that GQA captures near-MQA KV savings while staying more stable in quality; Shazeer [Shazeer2019MQA] is better treated as an extreme compression lower bound than a default.
2. If the target context is 128K or above and serving cost matters, prioritize local/global hybrids rather than going all-global. Start with Gemma 3’s [Gemma3Report] 5:1 interleave or a Mistral-style [Mistral2023] SWA recipe, then check whether the task truly needs more global layers. Otherwise memory and bandwidth saturate before quality becomes the real discussion.
3. Do not force MLA into small models just because it looks more advanced. A better boundary is MoE or dense models at or above 200B, where DeepSeek-V2-style [DeepSeekV2] latent-KV compression becomes worth considering; budget for decoupled RoPE, kernels, and serving-stack changes as part of the decision. If the team lacks that systems capacity, GQA/SWA usually has a better return.
4. Collapse Norm / FFN choices directly to pre-RMSNorm + SwiGLU. Do not spend large-scale benchmarking budget on LayerNorm, GELU, or post-norm unless the goal is historical reproduction. This follows from the combined evidence of Zhang and Sennrich [RMSNorm2019], Jiang et al. [Jiang2023PreRMSNorm], and Shazeer [Shazeer2020GLU].
5. Turn on QK-Norm first in two settings: when attention-logit spikes appear at scale, and during long-context continued pretraining. Wortsman et al. [Wortsman2023Instabilities] and Gemma 3 [Gemma3Report] are already enough to support this practice. Do not expect it to be a universal gain component at every scale.
6. If a stable base already exists, the target size is roughly ≤30B, and the continued-pretraining token budget is in the 50B–300B range, evaluate depth-up-scaling or block expansion before deciding on from-scratch training. SOLAR [Kim2023Solar] and LLaMA Pro [LLaMAPro2024] show that this path is often cheaper. Conversely, for a new architecture or a new large-scale data roadmap, planning directly under scaling laws is cleaner [Kaplan2020ScalingLaws][Llama3Report].
7. Do not run single-point ablations. Use at least 3 scale points, 2 context lengths, and report training stability, KV-cache, throughput, and downstream quality together. Otherwise it is easy to misattribute data-engineering gains to architecture, or serving gains to training [Fu2024DataEngineering][Fu2024PeakPerf].
8. Mark the evidence gaps explicitly: whether sandwich norm is necessary, whether QK-Norm delivers gains beyond stabilization, and how Transformers compare to SSM/RetNet under matched budgets all remain unresolved in clean public evidence
