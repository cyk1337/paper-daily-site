4:T5e5,Bottom line: “repetition” in pretraining is not a single knob but three different objects—(i) passive scraped repetition (mirrors, templates, syndication, cross-corpus reuse), (ii) active scheduled repetition (multi-epoch training on a finite pool), and (iii) zero-repeat subsets coupled to evaluation/legal/privacy constraints. Collapsing them into one pipeline ties together four goals—quality, memorization, contamination, and compliance—forcing blunt over-dedup or overly conservative policies. The evidence is consistent on the split: Lee et al. [Lee2021Dedup] and Tirumala et al. [Tirumala2023D4] show aggressive dedup on web corpora often improves perplexity while reducing leakage; Muennighoff et al. [Muennighoff2023DataConstrained] shows that on a “bleached” high-quality pool, uniform repetition is close to “fresh-token equivalent” up to ~4 epochs; Hernandez et al. [Hernandez2022RepeatedData] provides the counterexample—non-uniform repetition (a hot subset over-exposed) leaves a mechanistic fingerprint in induction heads and yields measurable degradation. A pragmatic engineering decomposition follows: (i) strict dedup + cross-source fingerprinting for scraped repetition; (ii) allow 2–4 epochs with a hot-subset probe; (iii) count exposures for benchmarks/copyright/PII, defaulting to 0–1 with provenance audits. Synthetic/self-consuming loops are a fourth “semantic repetition” regime where the classic 4-epoch heuristic may not apply and evidence is still thin.5:T4e4,Actionable takeaways: don’t treat “dedup vs repetition” as a binary choice. For passive scraped repetition, aggressive dedup (substring/MinHash as a baseline, semantic near-dup when needed) often reduces perplexity and leakage risk [Lee2021Dedup][Tirumala2023D4], and cross-corpus audits are required because the same documents recur across public corpora [Elazar2023WhatsInMyBigData]. For a “bleached” finite high-quality pool, uniform multi-epoch training is close to fresh-token equivalent up to ~4 epochs, with rapidly diminishing returns after that [Muennighoff2023DataConstrained]; but this does not apply to hot-subset over-exposure—non-uniform repetition causes measurable degradation and leaves mechanistic fingerprints in induction heads [Hernandez2022RepeatedData]. For benchmarks/copyright/PII, exposure counting is safer: default to 0–1 exposure with provenance + prefix/suffix dedup to control contamination [Deng2023BenchmarkContamination]; repeated exposure increases memorization risk, which also grows with model size and longer context [Carlini2022Memorization]. Synthetic self-consuming loops are a different risk surface; the “4 epochs” heuristic should not be blindly transferred [Shumailov2023CurseOfRecursion].6:T5f2,A common engineering mistake is to treat “repetition” as a single continuous knob: either dedup everything or accept multi-epoch training. A more stable decomposition optimizes three different objects. (1) Passive scraped repetition: syndication, mirrors, templates, pagination artifacts, and cross-corpus reuse. Lee et al. [Lee2021Dedup] quantifies double-digit near-dup rates in major corpora and shows substring/MinHash dedup improves perplexity while reducing memorization/leakage; Elazar et al. [Elazar2023WhatsInMyBigData] shows cross-corpus duplication breaks within-corpus dedup, motivating cross-source fingerprinting and global audits. (2) Active scheduled uniform repetition (epochs): Muennighoff et al. [Muennighoff2023DataConstrained] provides a data-limited curve—up to ~4 epochs repeated tokens are close to fresh-token equivalent, with rapidly diminishing equivalence beyond that. (3) Zero-repeat subsets coupled to evaluation/legal/privacy: Deng et al. [Deng2023BenchmarkContamination] shows benchmark contamination is hard to eradicate via broad filtering; Carlini et al. [Carlini2022Memorization] links repeated exposure to measurable memorization risk. The practical pipeline is therefore not “one pool to rule them all” but three lanes: strict bleaching for passive duplicates; 2–4 epochs on the bleached high-quality pool; and exposure-counted, separately audited sensitive/eval data [Lee2021Dedup][Muennighoff2023DataConstrained][Deng2023BenchmarkContamination][Carlini2022Memorization].7:T539,The key contribution of Muennighoff et al. This matches scaling-law intuition in data-limited regimes: once capacity can fit the training distribution, additional compute depends more on effective data and coverage [Kaplan2020ScalingLaws][Hernandez2021TransferScaling]. Hernandez et al. [Hernandez2022RepeatedData] provides the more dangerous engineering counterpoint: if exposure is non-uniform (e.g., 2% hot subset repeated 100×), even with the same total token budget, loss degrades measurably and induction heads show interpretable “repetition learning” fingerprints. Combined, an operational criterion is: don’t only track average epochs; track the exposure distribution’s tail (e.g., Gini/long-tail). Uniform repetition mainly increases utilization, while hot-subset over-exposure locks gradient budget onto a few fragments, hurting generalization and increasing memorization risk [Hernandez2022RepeatedData][Carlini2022Memorization].8:T5a5,“Aggressive dedup” is not the same as “delete as much as possible”; the key is whether you remove redundancy or information. SemDeDup targets MinHash’s blind spot: much redundancy is semantic rather than verbatim, and embedding-similarity dedup can match performance with fewer samples [Abbas2023SemDeDup]. D4 extends the goal from “dedup” to “dedup + diversification”: representative sampling within semantic clusters effectively increases coverage under a fixed token budget [Tirumala2023D4]. In contrast, mixture/reweighting (e.g., DoReMi, importance resampling) answers “where should the remaining budget go?”—they don’t directly remove duplicates but shift the training distribution toward a target distribution, reaching downstream-optimal regions faster at fixed compute [Xie2023DoReMi][Xie2023ImportanceResampling]. Pruning is closer to cost compression, but large-scale results are not stable: Marion et al.
