4:T5b4,Tokenizers used to be treated as “preprocessing”: ensure coverage and minimize bytes/token. Controlled evidence from the last three years pushes it back into a first-class spec. At fixed compute, tokenizer choice alone explains 0.6–5.1 pp downstream variance [Ali2024TokenizerChoice]. Scaling vocab from 32K to 128K adds near-zero inference overhead at large-model serving, yet reduces training loss by 0.02–0.04 nats and materially improves bytes/token for non-English and code [Dubey2024Llama3]. More importantly, tokenization is not just compression: digit tokenization yields persistent 10–20 pp gaps on 3–5 digit arithmetic that do not close with scale [Spiess2023Digits][Singh2024TokenizationCounts]. Under-trained tokens are widespread in deployed LMs and can emit garbage at generation time, motivating an end-of-run sweep and repair loop [LandBartolo2024Magikarp]. Cross-tokenizer comparisons based on per-token perplexity conflate “shorter tokens” with “better modeling”; bits-per-byte (BPB) is the more defensible normalization [Gao2020Pile]. Tokenizer-free (byte/char/patch) lines are at or near BPB parity with BPE [Xue2021ByT5][Yu2023Megabyte][Pagnoni2024BLT], but a 2–3× inference tax keeps them from being the default production choice in 2026. Cross-tokenizer comparisons should be byte-normalized (BPB); per-token perplexity confounds “shorter tokens” with “better modeling” [Gao2020Pile]. Tokenizer-free lines (ByT5/MEGABYTE/BLT) are near BPB parity, but a 2–3× inference tax makes them better as research or niche solutions than the 2026 default production path [Xue2021ByT5][Yu2023Megabyte][Pagnoni2024BLT].6:T52e,A pragmatic shared premise: tokenizers are not primarily compressors; they decide which patterns become atoms and which must be composed by the model. This is why fertility/bytes-per-token alone often misleads. Under fixed compute, Schmidt et al. [Schmidt2024TokenizationMoreThanCompression] show that more compressed tokenizers are not consistently better downstream, because morphology alignment, domain coverage, and token frequency distributions reshape gradient sparsity and the compositional structures that generalize. Hiraoka et al. [Hiraoka2024SurfaceInfoTokens] adds a representational angle: embeddings encode token surface-form information (length/shape), so tokenization directly changes the representational atoms.

Ali et al. [Ali2024TokenizerChoice] turns this into controlled evidence: holding model size and training budget constant, swapping tokenizers yields 0.6–5.1 pp downstream variance. The “cheapness” comes from the fact that tokenizer changes barely touch core system costs (kernels, parallelism, KV cache), yet systematically alter sequence-length distributions, the learnability of rare tokens, and cross-domain/cross-lingual coverage. Treating tokenizers as constants effectively defers a controllable error source to later, where it is paid with more compute or more complex post-training.7:T5f0,A more stable reading of vocab scaling: it shifts part of the modeling burden from “composing over longer sequences” to “a larger embedding table with more direct substring coverage.” Llama 3 reports that moving from 32K to 128K reduces training loss by ~0.02–0.04 nats and provides fertility-style stats [Dubey2024Llama3]. DeepSeek-V3 makes tokenizer design explicit and reports BPB-style tables to make gains comparable [DeepSeekV3TechReport2024]. Together they point to an engineering fact: once the model size and serving stack are optimized around large batches and KV cache, the incremental cost of a 128K vocab is mostly embedding/softmax parameters and bandwidth—not the dominant inference compute bottleneck—while shorter sequences directly reduce attention-related costs.

However, “bigger vocab is always better” is not a universal rule. Schmidt et al. [Schmidt2024TokenizationMoreThanCompression] cautions that compression is not sufficient; larger vocabs can also create longer tails, thinning training signal. This risk is amplified by Magikarp-style diagnostics: under-trained tokens often concentrate in low-frequency, morphologically odd, or cross-domain stitched tokens [LandBartolo2024Magikarp]. Hence, the reproducible gain from 32K→128K is better framed as “finer coverage of common substrings under a reasonable sampling/filtering pipeline,” not as a mandate to push blindly to 256K+. Public fixed-compute/latency evidence for 128K→256K remains a gap (see open questions).8:T66e,Digits provide the cleanest counterexample: they are nearly semantics-free and mostly test compositional and positional generalization. Spiess et al. [Spiess2023Digits] show single-digit tokenization beats merged BPE by 10–20 pp on 3–5 digit arithmetic, with no convergence from 1B to 7B; Singh et al. [Singh2024TokenizationCounts] observes the same ordering across GPT-4, Claude, and Gemini, indicating this is not merely “small models undertrained” but a boundary choice that turns multi-digit numbers into harder discrete-vocabulary memorization. Nogueira et al. [Nogueira2021ArithmeticLimits] also supports, from earlier arithmetic tasks, that number surface forms change learnability.

In multilingual settings, wrong boundaries are more subtle: rather than a single benchmark drop, some languages are systematically assigned worse representational atoms. Limisiewicz et al. [Limisiewicz2023VocabAllocation] shows vocabulary allocation and cross-language overlap affect effective capacity for low-resource languages; Toraman et al. [Toraman2022TurkishTokenization] demonstrates sensitivity for morphologically rich Turkish. Code brings whitespace boundaries to the foreground: indentation and alignment are part of syntax, and merging spaces with neighbors forces extra compositional burden to learn indentation patterns—motivations that recur in code datasets and code LMs [Kocetkov2022TheStack][Guo2024DeepSeekCoder]. Taken together, a unified mechanism emerges: wrong boundaries turn compositional structures (digits, morphemes, indents) into sparse long-tail tokens, creating capability gaps that scale does not reliably erase.9:T5d5,The most common technical confusion in cross-tokenizer discussions is comparing per-token PPL/CE: shorter tokens mechanically reduce per-token loss, which is not equivalent to better modeling of the original text (byte sequence). A more defensible approach is byte-normalized loss, i.e., BPB. Byte-based accounting and comparison already appears in The Pile’s engineering narrative [Gao2020Pile] and is repeatedly used as a shared coordinate system in later tokenizer studies [Ali2024TokenizerChoice].

Under this coordinate system, tokenizer-free progress is easier to place. ByT5 shows byte-to-byte pretraining can approach subword quality while offering structural advantages in noise robustness and multilingual coverage [Xue2021ByT5]. MEGABYTE pushes byte-level modeling to longer sequences via a multiscale architecture [Yu2023Megabyte]. BLT uses entropy-based dynamic patching to reach BPE parity in BPB and explicitly reports a 2–3× inference cost [Pagnoni2024BLT]. Hence, the pragmatic 2026 decision is less “tokenizer-free or not” and more “under which product constraints is a 2–3× throughput tax worth paying”—e.g., extreme multilingual/noisy inputs, high OOV sensitivity, or unified pipelines over text plus binary fragments.
