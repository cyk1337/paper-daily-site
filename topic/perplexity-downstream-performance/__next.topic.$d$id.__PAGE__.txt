4:T601,This literature cluster studies a practical engineering question: perplexity is the cheapest and most stable metric during pretraining, but downstream task performance is what actually decides whether to keep training, compress, or ship a model. Earlier work such as Kaplan et al. [Kaplan2020ScalingLaws] and Hoffmann et al. [Hoffmann2022Chinchilla] made loss/PPL the central variable in scaling laws and showed strong predictability. Later work, however, shows that this predictability mostly holds in a narrow regime: same tokenizer, same language distribution, same objective, same model family, and no strong post-training intervention. Once we cross tokenizers, languages, architectures, alignment stages, or enter compression and sparsification settings, PPL and downstream capability decouple in systematic ways. Gadre et al. [Gadre2024OvertrainingDownstream], Bhagia et al. [Bhagia2024TaskScalingLadders], and Isik et al. [Isik2024DownstreamScaling] move the field toward treating PPL as an intermediate variable inside task-scaling laws rather than the final decision variable. Khanal and Capone [KhanalCapone2024CompressionTasks], Liu et al. [HongLiu2022SameLossBetterDownstream], and Schaeffer et al. [Schaeffer2023Mirage] further show that a single scalar hides behavioral divergence, discrete thresholds, and implicit bias. A more robust engineering conclusion is: keep PPL/BPB for monitoring and fitting inside the training loop, but use task-scaling laws plus a multi-panel diagnostic suite for decisions outside the loop.5:T69a,Bottom line first: PPL is still useful, but it is reliable mainly inside the training loop. It works well for early stopping, anomaly detection, data-mix tuning, and compute-optimal fitting because those settings share tokenization, objective, and evaluation protocol, and PPL is the cheapest signal available [Kaplan2020ScalingLaws][Hoffmann2022Chinchilla][Porian2024ResolvingScaling]. Once the question becomes 'is this model actually better at tasks, safe to compress, or ready to ship?', a single PPL number is not enough. There are three recurring failure modes. First, PPL is not a common unit across tokenizers or languages; vocabulary size and segmentation directly change the number, so raw PPL is not comparable and should at least be replaced by bits-per-byte or information-normalized measures [Tao2024VocabularyScaling][Luitel2024NepaliTokenizationPPL]. Second, compression, sparsification, alignment, and fine-tuning can induce larger behavioral changes than token-level average loss changes; Khanal and Capone [KhanalCapone2024CompressionTasks] and Hong Liu et al. [HongLiu2022SameLossBetterDownstream] both provide counterexamples where similar loss yields different behavior. Third, many discrete tasks have thresholds and phase transitions, and smooth PPL curves can hide those jumps [Schaeffer2023Mirage][Schaeffer2024WhyElusive]. The more robust approach is not to discard PPL, but to place it in stage one of a task-scaling pipeline: use PPL/BPB to monitor training, then use per-task scaling laws and a standardized evaluation panel to decide whether to continue training, compress, or ship [Gadre2024OvertrainingDownstream][Bhagia2024TaskScalingLadders][Gu2024OLMES].6:T560,If the question stays inside the pretraining loop, PPL is indeed a good metric. The shared assumption behind Kaplan et al. [Kaplan2020ScalingLaws], Hoffmann et al. [Hoffmann2022Chinchilla], and Henighan et al. [Henighan2020AutoregressiveScaling] is not that PPL equals intelligence, but that within the same tokenizer, objective, and nearby data distribution, cross-entropy loss follows stable power laws with respect to parameters, data, and compute, making small-to-large extrapolation possible. In that regime, the advantages of PPL are concrete: it is cheap, low-noise, available throughout training, and sensitive enough to recipe changes to support early stopping, anomaly detection, data-mixture tuning, and compute allocation. Porian et al. [Porian2024ResolvingScaling] further show that even differences between Kaplan and Chinchilla often come from protocol details such as warm-up, token accounting, and tokenization rather than a breakdown of loss scaling itself. This matters because PPL is strongest as a controlled relative-comparison tool, not as a universal capability score. The more stable reading is therefore not to reject PPL, but to bound its domain of validity: same family, same protocol, inside the training loop, it remains the most cost-effective leftmost dashboard panel [Kaplan2020ScalingLaws][Hoffmann2022Chinchilla][Porian2024ResolvingScaling].7:T65c,When PPL fails as a downstream proxy, three mechanisms recur. The first is non-comparable units. Once Tao et al. [Tao2024VocabularyScaling] include vocabulary size in scaling laws, vocabulary is shown to systematically change both PPL and the optimal configuration; Luitel et al. [Luitel2024NepaliTokenizationPPL] further show in Nepali that finer segmentation often lowers PPL without reliably improving fine-tuned performance. Raw PPL is therefore not a common unit across tokenizers or languages. The second mechanism is behavioral rewriting with little change in average token loss. Hong Liu et al. [HongLiu2022SameLossBetterDownstream] show that downstream performance can differ at the same pretraining loss because optimization path and implicit bias matter; Khanal and Capone [KhanalCapone2024CompressionTasks] provide an even sharper engineering counterexample in compression: at high sparsity, PPL barely moves while task scores fall, and JS divergence tracks behavioral drift better. The third mechanism is discrete thresholds. Schaeffer et al. [Schaeffer2023Mirage] argue against a naive story of sudden emergence by showing that smooth underlying improvements can look discontinuous after metric transformation; Schaeffer et al. [Schaeffer2024WhyElusive] then explain why MCQ and frontier-capability prediction remain difficult. Taken together, the conclusion is straightforward: PPL is not wrong, but it only observes average token-level coding efficiency, not behavioral structure directly [Tao2024VocabularyScaling][HongLiu2022SameLossBetterDownstream][KhanalCapone2024CompressionTasks][Schaeffer2024WhyElusive].8:T5fa,The more practical move is not 'stop using PPL,' but 'stop letting PPL decide on its own.' The common direction in Gadre et al. [Gadre2024OvertrainingDownstream], Bhagia et al. [Bhagia2024TaskScalingLadders], and Krajewski et al. [Krajewski2025DownstreamMetricsScaling] is to model task scores directly: some use loss as an intermediate state before mapping to tasks, while others fit multi-task log-accuracy directly from training budget. Their difference from the Kaplan line [Kaplan2020ScalingLaws] is not that loss contains no signal, but that task scores are not simply a monotonic reparameterization of loss. Isik et al. [Isik2024DownstreamScaling] already observe this non-monotonicity in transfer settings; Liu et al. [LeiLiu2025PerplexityAwareCPT] use perplexity landscapes for CPT data selection, showing that PPL still works as a domain-match signal but should not overreach into a final utility criterion. An executable workflow is therefore: stage one, under fixed tokenization and protocol, use PPL/BPB for training monitoring, anomaly detection, data mixing, and compute allocation; stage two, fit task-scaling laws directly on at least 20 representative tasks and use a standardized evaluation framework such as OLMES [Gu2024OLMES] to keep comparison protocol fixed.
